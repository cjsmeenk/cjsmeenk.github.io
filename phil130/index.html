Course Description

In this course we will study the nature of space and time. We begin with foundational questions about motion and geometry: Zeno’s paradoxes challenge our understanding of continuous motion, while the development of non-Euclidean geometries in the 19th century raises deep questions about how we can know the geometrical structure of space. We then turn to disputes regarding the nature of space, time, and motion waged in the seventeenth century by the likes of Descartes, Newton, and Leibniz. We will study the debate between “absolute” and “relational” accounts of space and time. Relationalists treat space as consisting of nothing but the spatial relationships between objects, whereas proponents of “absolute” space assert that space exists as a substance in its own right. We next turn to Einstein’s special and general theories of relativity, the new conceptions of space and time they introduce, and how they transform the classical debates. The final part of the course turns to questions regarding the nature of time in contemporary physics.
