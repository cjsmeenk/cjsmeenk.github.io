Empiricism and the Philosophy of Physics

Theory-Mediated Measurement and Effective Field Theories
Instructor: Chris Smeenk  ·  Time: W 2:00–4:50  ·  Location: Bunche 2173
Office Hours: TBD  ·  Contact: chris.smeenk@gmail.com
How should we characterize the structure and content of physical theories? Many philosophers of science have analyzed scientific theories using mathematical logic, following the logical positivists. This seminar considers recent work in philosophy of physics that takes a different starting point: how theories are actually applied, and the mathematical tools—approximation methods, perturbative techniques, scaling arguments, iterative refinement—used in those applications. This shift in focus, from formal logical structure to the practices of approximation, measurement, and scale-dependent modeling, reveals aspects of theory structure that earlier accounts ignored.

First we consider an account of the content of theories that takes theory-mediated measurement as a guiding idea. This raises a threat of circularity: the measurement of theoretical quantities presupposes the very theory that measurement is meant to test. This apparent circularity can be resolved through an iterative, eliminative methodology, exemplified in historical cases. In the second half of the seminar, we consider how this picture relates to the framework of effective field theories (EFTs), which has transformed how physicists understand the scope and validity of physical theories. The EFT perspective suggests that theories are valid within limited domains, rather than providing complete descriptions of possible worlds.

Participation (20%)

Active seminar participation based on careful reading and reflection. Half is based on contributions over the term; the other half on a 30-minute presentation on a topic of your choice.

Commentaries (10%)

Post short commentaries (250–500 words) on the assigned readings by 5:00 p.m. Monday each week on the Discussion tab. You may skip one week without penalty.

Paper (70%)

A research paper of 6,000–7,500 words. A topic proposal and bibliography are due in Week 7; the final paper is due the week after the last meeting.

Background

The seminar does not presume extensive background in physics. EFT material is introduced at a conceptual level, and we develop the necessary ideas together.

Part I · From Duhem to Theory-Mediated Measurement
Week 1Duhem’s Aim and Structure›

Assigned

  • Duhem, The Aim and Structure of Physical Theory: Part I, Ch. 1–4; Part II, Ch. 6

Optional

  • Ariew & Barker, Introduction to Essays in the History and Philosophy of Science
  • Stump, “Pierre Duhem’s Virtue Epistemology”
Week 2Coordination and the Problem of Measurement›

How do theoretical quantities get their empirical content? Measurement seems to require the theory it is meant to test, prompting worries about circularity. We examine the conventionalist response and Chang’s account of “epistemic iteration.”

Assigned

  • van Fraassen, Scientific Representation, Chapters 5–6
  • Chang, Inventing Temperature, Chapter 5

Optional

  • Poincaré, “The Measure of Time”
  • Reichenbach, The Philosophy of Space and Time (selections)
Week 3Theory-Mediated Measurement›

The central methodological proposal: we avoid circularity through iterative, theory-mediated measurement. We examine how celestial mechanics exemplifies this approach.

Assigned

  • Smith, “Closing the Loop”

Optional

  • Smith, “The Methodology of the Principia” (2002)
  • Kuhn, “The Function of Measurement in Modern Physical Science”
Week 4Case Study — Molecular Reality›

How did convergent measurements of Avogadro’s number establish molecular reality? This case study illustrates the iterative, eliminative methodology.

Assigned

  • Smith & Seth, Brownian Motion and Molecular Reality, Ch. 6–7 and Postscript
  • Demopoulos, On Theories, Chapter 2
  • van Fraassen, “The Perils of Perrin”

Optional

  • Achinstein, “Was There Evidence for Atoms?”
  • Coko, “Jean Perrin and the Philosophers’ Stories”
Week 5Data, Phenomena, and Measurement›

What do measurements establish? Bogen and Woodward’s distinction between data and phenomena helps clarify evidential reasoning: theories explain and predict phenomena, which are inferred from data but not directly observed.

Assigned

  • Bogen & Woodward, “Saving the Phenomena,” Philosophical Review (1988)
  • Woodward, “Data and Phenomena: A Restatement and Defense,” Synthese (2011)

Optional

  • Tal, “How Accurate is the Standard Second?”
  • Massimi, “From Data to Phenomena”
Part II · Effective Field Theories and the Structure of Theories
Week 6Physics Avoidance and Scales›

Philosophers often take the content of a theory to be specified by a set of possible worlds. We consider Wilson’s critique, which emphasizes how terms shift meaning across contexts and scales, and why the “possible worlds” approach systematically misdescribes how theories work.

Assigned

  • Wilson, Physics Avoidance, Chapter 2 (“The Greediness of Scales”)

Optional

  • Fraser, review of Physics Avoidance, Philosophy of Science (2021)
  • Ryckman, review in NDPR
Week 7Introducing Effective Field Theories›

The EFT framework has transformed how physicists understand the scope and validity of theories. We introduce renormalization, decoupling, and domain-restricted validity at a conceptual level.

Assigned

  • Wells, Effective Theories in Physics, Chapters 1–2
  • Hartmann, “Effective Field Theories, Reductionism, and Scientific Explanation”

Optional

  • Georgi, “Effective Field Theory”
  • Williams, “Renormalization Group Methods: A Primer for Philosophers”
Week 8EFT and the Structure of Theories›

The EFT framework provides an account of theory structure and content that differs strikingly from standard philosophical views. We explore connections to Wilson’s critique, Ruetsche’s account of interpretation, and Williams’s effective realism.

Assigned

  • Wilson, TBD
  • Ruetsche, Interpreting Quantum Theories: §1.1–1.5, §15.1–15.2, 15.5–6
  • Williams, “Scientific Realism Made Effective”
Week 9EFT, Measurement, and Scale›

What do measurements probe in the EFT picture? Quantities at accessible scales, not “fundamental” quantities. We explore how Wilson’s semantic point—that terms refer differently at different scales—connects to the EFT framework and to measurement.

Assigned

  • Wilson, Physics Avoidance, Chapter 3 (“Semantic Mimicry”)
  • Williams, “Naturalness, the Autonomy of Scales, and the 125 GeV Higgs”

Optional

  • Wilson, Imitation of Rigor (selections)
  • Ruetsche, “Perils of Approximate Ontology”
Week 10Effective Realism and Its Critics›

Can we be realists about EFT-based physics without interpreting theories in the traditional sense? We examine effective realism and Ruetsche’s empiricist challenge. What epistemic stance does the measurement-centered, EFT-informed view support?

Assigned

  • Ruetsche, “Renormalization Group Realism: The Ascent of Pessimism”
  • Ruetsche, “Perturbing Realism”
  • Woodward, “Explanation, Truth and Structural Realism” (.docx)

Optional

  • Fraser, “Renormalization and the Formulation of Scientific Realism”
  • Ruetsche, “The Perils of Approximate Ontology”
  • Saatsi, “(In)Effective Realism?”
  • Miller, “Worldly Imprecision”
